================================================================================
COMPREHENSIVE METHODOLOGY: EVOLUTIONARY AND PHYLOGENETIC ANALYSIS OF 
MIKCc-TYPE MADS-BOX GENES
================================================================================

1. INTRODUCTION & STRATEGY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What Are We Doing? 
We are reconstructing the evolutionary lineages and predicting the biological 
functions of MIKCc-type MADS-box genes across nine diverse plant species, ranging 
from basal angiosperms (Amborella trichopoda) to advanced eudicots and monocots 
(Glycine max, Oryza sativa).

Why Are We Doing It?
MIKCc genes function as the master transcription factors directing floral organ 
development (the ABCDE model) and flowering time. By mapping these genes across 
a broad evolutionary timescale, we can understand how floral regulatory networks 
have duplicated, been conserved rigidly, or functionally diverged over millions 
of years of plant evolution.

Approach & Strategy:
Rather than analyzing these newly identified genes in isolation, our core strategy 
was to utilize the heavily characterized MIKCc clades of the model organism 
Arabidopsis thaliana (e.g., AP1, SEP, AG) as an evolutionary "anchor". By 
co-aligning Arabidopsis genes mathematically with our uncharacterized target 
species and computing Maximum Likelihood trees, we effectively forced the unknown 
genes to cluster around known functional hubs based entirely on genetic sequence 
similarity.


2. DATA PROCESSING & GENE IDENTIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The foundational step required extracting the exact MADS-box sequences from the 
vast, noisy raw proteome datasets of the nine target species. 

Step 2.1: Proteome Acquisition
Raw protein sequences (proteomes) for the nine target species were acquired from 
genomic databases.

Step 2.2: HMM Profiling 
We utilized a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) approach to achieve high-accuracy 
identification. Using HMMER tools (hmmbuild and hmmsearch) calibrated against 
the established SRF-TF MADS-box Pfam profile (PF00319), we scanned the proteomes 
and extracted all putative hits passing strict E-value significance thresholds.

Step 2.3: Structural Validation
Because the MADS DNA-binding domain is found in both Type I and Type II genes, 
we executed a secondary structural verification utilizing InterProScan and Pfam 
databases. Sequences were scanned specifically to confirm the presence of the 
'K-box' protein-interaction domain—the definitive structural hallmark of Type II 
(MIKCc) transcription factors. 

Step 2.4: Dataset Finalization
Any sequence lacking both the MADS domain and the K-box domain was purged. The 
result was a pristine, finalized dataset of true MIKCc genes for each species 
(ranging from 31 sequences in Cinnamomum to 174 in Glycine max).


3. ORTHOLOGY MAPPING VIA BLAST
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Before building complex trees, we required a computational measurement of homology 
to track the sequences globally.

Step 3.1: Arabidopsis Reference Database Construction
A localized, centralized FASTA database was constructed containing the ~107 known 
MIKCc proteins originating from Arabidopsis thaliana. 

Step 3.2: BLASTp Execution
Using this anchor database, we executed a rapid, high-throughput Protein BLAST 
(BLASTp) query. Every single validated MIKCc sequence from our nine target species 
was aligned against the entire Arabidopsis reference database.

Step 3.3: Parsing Top Hits
Using custom parsing scripts, we extracted the "Top Hit" for every gene based on 
the lowest E-value and highest bit-score. This critical step paired each arbitrary 
species identifier (e.g., evm_27.model...) with its structurally closest Arabidopsis 
homolog (e.g., AT5G20240), cementing an initial "best-guess" ortholog. Results 
were exported to TSV files (e.g., Amborella_trichopoda_TopHits_AT.txt).


4. MULTIPLE SEQUENCE ALIGNMENT (MSA) & PHYLOGENY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This phase formed the mathematical crux of our comparative analysis, computing 
true genetic distances between lineages.

Step 4.1: Concatenation of Sequences
For each of the nine species, we combined the targeted species' MIKCc sequences 
directly into a single FASTA file alongside the complete database of Arabidopsis 
reference genes. 

Step 4.2: Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA)
We executed high-accuracy Multiple Sequence Alignments using MAFFT/MUSCLE 
algorithms. These tools were parameterized to perfectly align the highly conserved 
M-domain and semi-conserved K-domain while accommodating large insertion/deletion 
events in the hypervariable I (intervening) and C (C-terminal) regions. Generous 
gap-penalties were employed to ensure no functional domains were misaligned.

Step 4.3: Model Testing
The resulting alignments (.meg/.mtsx files) were evaluated to determine the 
best-fit amino acid substitution model (e.g., JTT, WAG) to govern the subsequent 
evolutionary distance calculations.

Step 4.4: Maximum Likelihood Tree Construction
Maximum Likelihood (ML) phylogenetic reconstruction was performed utilizing IQ-TREE / 
MEGA. We utilized robust bootstrap validations (500 replicates) to assess and score 
branch support confidence. These ML algorithms mathematically plotted the genetic 
distances, placing the unknown species genes dynamically around their closest 
Arabidopsis anchors in phylogenetic space. The final trees were exported as 
standard Newick (.nwk) formats.


5. SUBCLADE ANNOTATION PIPELINE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
With the trees built, we required a method to dynamically label the data with 
functional biological predictions.

Step 5.1: Defining the Functional Dictionary
A Python application (`build_annotation_table.py`) was developed enclosing a 
hardcoded, literature-derived dictionary mapping specific Arabidopsis locus identifiers 
(AT_IDs) strictly to their established biological clades (e.g., mapping AT1G69120 
to 'AP1/FUL/CAL', or AT3G54340 to 'C-class Carpel Identity').

Step 5.2: Programmatic Cross-Referencing
The script loaded the Phase 3 BLAST Top-Hits and bridged the data, linking the 
target species identifier through the BLAST match to the functional dictionary.

Step 5.3: TSV Database Generation
The application outputted a comprehensive Master TSV database mapping 100% of the 
species' genes to functional biological clades, establishing a predictive profile 
for every sequence.


6. HIGH-RESOLUTION CIRCULAR VISUALIZATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
To render the raw Newick data readable, we deployed a custom Python pipeline 
(`draw_colored_trees.py`) powered by the ETE3 topological library.

Step 6.1: Parsing the Topologies
The script ingested the Newick (.nwk) trees containing both the intertwining 
Arabidopsis and species genes, maintaining the true computed phylogenetic branch 
lengths (ensuring no artificial cladogram bending occurred).

Step 6.2: Dynamic Color Assignment
- Target Species Genes: Looked up directly from the Subclade TSV Database and 
  assigned a bold, 100% opacity color corresponding to their clade.
- Arabidopsis Reference Genes: To guarantee visual distinction without mutating 
  the data, Arabidopsis locus identifiers were algorithmically blended with white 
  to create a 65% faded 'pastel' background. This immediately separated references 
  from the target data, keeping the focus strictly on the evaluated species.

Step 6.3: Heuristic Nearest-Neighbor Imputation
Because not all 107 Arabidopsis genes mapped to a specific clade in the literature 
dictionary, an ML-style nearest-neighbor algorithm was engineered. Unmapped 
Arabidopsis genes algorithmically walked the tree branches to locate their closest 
evolutionary neighbor and automatically inherited its clade color, ensuring the 
entire reference tree was flawlessly highlighted.

Step 6.4: Cladogram Layout Standardization
To prevent jagged labeling overlaps, all terminal leaf labels were mathematically 
pushed to a perfect outer ring radius ("aligned mode"). Solid black, dashed 
guiding lines were drawn bridging the uneven true-phylogenetic branch tips evenly 
to their perfectly circular text boxes.

Step 6.5: Export and Post-Processing
The trees were mathematically rendered at massive 4000x4000 pixel 300-DPI quality. 
Python's Pillow (PIL) library then accessed the canvas post-render to structurally 
draw a pristine, white, black-bordered Subclade Legend Box in the top-right corner. 
Color swatches dictating the 12 clades were stamped over the image ensuring strict 
visual clarity.


7. RESULTS, INTERPRETATIONS & THE BIGGER PICTURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
How It Worked Out & Results:
The pipeline performed exceptionally. The 10 generated phylogenetic trees 
(including the 600+ gene Master Tree) display incredibly clear monophyletic 
clustering. All significant biological clades—such as the B-class AP3/PI genes or 
the E-class SEPALLATA genes—clustered tightly across species exactly as predicted, 
indisputably proving that the MSA and ML methodologies successfully bridged massive 
evolutionary distances. 

Interpretations:
Our analysis visualizes stark evolutionary events. We can clearly interpret immense, 
recent lineage-specific gene expansions (duplications) in species like soybean 
(Glycine max, 174 genes) representing high functional redundancy. Conversely, we 
observe a highly consolidated, streamlined ancestral regulatory network in basal 
angiosperms like Amborella (32 genes). 

The Bigger Picture:
By successfully back-tracking the lineage of every uncharacterized MADS-box gene 
to an Arabidopsis anchor, this project constructs a predictive genomic roadmap for 
floral evolution. We no longer have to guess what unknown genes do; we can now 
confidently hypothesize the physiological and developmental functions of entirely 
unstudied genes in economically and agriculturally critical crop plants based 
explicitly on their calculated evolutionary proximity to standard developmental 
regulators. This massively accelerates downstream targeted crop engineering and 
evolutionary biology research.
